the problem

Your teammate's reasoning is trapped on their machine.

You and a teammate are both deep in Claude Code on the same codebase. You need to know what they decided about the schema, or why they picked one library over another. Today you have two bad options.

option 1

Ping them and wait

They're heads-down, three hours into a problem. You break their flow, or you sit blocked for forty-five minutes. Usually both.

option 2

Ask them to paste it

They dump the whole conversation into Slack — hundreds of lines of back-and-forth you scroll through, or paste into your own Claude until it chokes on the context.

what CodeBaton does

Their Claude becomes something you can just ask.

Type a question in plain language. Your Claude reaches into your teammate's live session, pulls the relevant context, and answers with citations — in seconds.

The session stays on their laptop. Nothing to host, no environment to spin up, no transcript to paste. They don't even get a notification — it's read-only and out of their way. You opt in per session and choose exactly who can see it; the moment the session ends, it's gone.

why not just read their commits

GitHub has the answer. CodeBaton has the why.

01

Most reasoning never reaches a commit

Engineers iterate dozens of times per commit. The dead ends, the experiments, the "wait, let me try X" — that's where the knowledge lives, and Git throws all of it away.

02

Commits show the decision, not the why

"Use cursor pagination" is a one-line diff. The ninety minutes of figuring out why offset broke first is what your teammate actually needs when they hit the same fork.

03

Reviewed code is sanitized

By the time it lands in main, the messy real reasoning has been edited out for "code quality." The questions, the alternatives, the almost-shipped bug — gone.

GitHub captures the finished work that survived review. CodeBaton captures the live reasoning that produced it — including what your teammate is figuring out this second.

how it works

Four slash commands. That's the whole surface.

/connect <teammate>
Link up. They /connect you back — mutual means trusted. Per session, you decide what's visible.
/ask <teammate> <question>
Query their live session in plain language. Cited answer in seconds, drawn only from what they actually did.
/shared
See exactly what you're sharing, with whom, right now.
/disconnect
Cut it off — one teammate or everyone. Their access to your context ends instantly.

faq

The honest answers.

Is my code safe?
Nothing is shared by default. You opt in one session at a time with /connect, and you name exactly who can see it. Only members of your GitHub org can connect at all. When the session ends — or you /disconnect — the shared copy is wiped.
Is this an autonomous cloud agent?
No. The AI is the Claude Code already running on each engineer's laptop. CodeBaton is the connective tissue between humans and their Claudes — no autonomous work, no cloud sandbox, nothing billed for inference by us.
What do I need to set up?
A GitHub account — that's your workspace, and how teammates are discovered. Plus Claude Code, which you already have. No new accounts, no manual invites, no infrastructure.
Does it interrupt my teammate?
Never. Queries are read-only and asynchronous. They don't get pinged, their session isn't touched, and their context window is untouched. They find out you asked only if they look.
